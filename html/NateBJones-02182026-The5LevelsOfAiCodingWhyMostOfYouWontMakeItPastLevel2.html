The 5 Levels of AI Coding (Why Most of You Won't Make It Past Level 2)

NateBJones - 02/18/2026
Summary
A small number of frontier teams are now running "dark factories" where AI agents autonomously write, test, and ship production software with zero human code authorship or review. Meanwhile, rigorous research shows that most developers using AI tools actually get 19% slower, not faster, because they bolt AI onto existing workflows instead of redesigning their processes from the ground up. This video walks through Dan Shapiro's five-level framework for AI coding maturity, from "spicy autocomplete" (Level 0) to fully autonomous software factories (Level 5), and argues that roughly 90% of self-described AI-native developers are stuck at Level 2 or below. The core thesis is that the gap between frontier teams and everyone else is not a technology gap but a people, culture, and organizational gap -- and closing it requires rethinking specifications, org structures, talent pipelines, and the very definition of what it means to be a software engineer.
Key Points
The paradox: frontier teams ship with zero human code, while most developers get measurably slower with AI

At Anthropic, 90% of Claude Code's codebase was written by Claude Code itself, and the company estimates it is converging toward 100% AI-generated code. StrongDM runs a three-person "software factory" where no human writes or reviews code. OpenAI reports that Codex 5.3 was instrumental in creating itself, with earlier builds identifying inefficiencies in the training process. These are not demos or press releases -- they represent real production software shipped autonomously by AI agents.

At the same time, a rigorous 2025 randomized controlled trial by METR found that experienced open-source developers using AI coding tools completed tasks 19% slower than those working without AI. Critically, those same developers believed AI had made them 24% faster. They were wrong about both the direction and magnitude of the change. This disconnect between perception and reality is one of the most important findings in recent AI research.

The gap between these two realities -- dark factories running lights-out and average teams slowing down while thinking they are speeding up -- is the central tension of the video. It is not a temporary mismatch that will self-correct. It reflects fundamentally different approaches to integrating AI into software development, and the distance is accelerating rather than shrinking.

Dan Shapiro's five levels of AI coding maturity, from autocomplete to dark factory

Level 0 Spicy Autocomplete. The AI suggests the next line and you accept or reject it. This is the original GitHub Copilot experience: a faster tab key. The human is still writing the software; AI merely reduces keystrokes.

Level 1 Coding Intern. You hand the AI a discrete, well-scoped task such as writing a function, building a component, or refactoring a module. You review everything it returns. The AI handles individual tasks, but the human owns architecture, judgment, and integration.

Level 2 Junior Developer. The AI handles multi-file changes, navigates a codebase, understands dependencies, and builds features spanning modules. You still read all the code. Shapiro estimates 90% of developers who call themselves AI-native are operating here. This is the level where people tend to overestimate their maturity -- they think they are further along than they actually are.

Level 3 Developer as Manager. The relationship flips. You are no longer writing code with AI help; you are directing AI and reviewing its output at the feature or pull-request level. The model submits PRs for your review. Most developers top out here because they struggle with the psychological difficulty of letting go of the code. Level 4 Developer as Product Manager. You write a specification, leave, and return hours later to check whether tests pass. The code is a black box. You care whether it works but do not read the implementation. This requires extreme trust in both the AI system and your ability to write a complete, unambiguous spec. Level 5 The Dark Factory. A black box that turns specifications into working software. No human writes code. No human reviews code. Specification goes in, working software comes out. Almost nobody on the planet operates here today.

StrongDM's software factory: the most documented example of Level 5 in production

StrongDM's factory is run by just three people -- Justin McCarthy (CTO), Jay Taylor, and Nan Chowan. They have operated it since July 2024, and the inflection point they identify is the release of Claude 3.5 Sonnet in late 2024, when long-horizon agentic coding started compounding correctness more than compounding errors. The factory runs on an open-source coding agent called Attractor, and the entire agent is defined by just three markdown specification files.

One of the most important innovations is their use of "scenarios" instead of traditional tests. Traditional tests live inside the codebase and can be seen by the AI agent, which means the agent can (intentionally or not) optimize for passing those tests rather than building correct software -- the same problem as teaching to the test in education. Scenarios live outside the codebase as behavioral specifications that describe what the software should do from an external perspective. The agent never sees the evaluation criteria, preventing it from gaming the system. This is conceptually identical to a "holdout set" in machine learning, used to prevent overfitting.

StrongDM also built a "digital twin universe": behavioral clones of every external service their software interacts with -- simulated Okta, Jira, Slack, Google Docs, Google Drive, and Google Sheets. AI agents develop against these twins, enabling full integration testing without touching real production systems, APIs, or data. Their output is real production software: CXDB, their AI context store, consists of 16,000 lines of Rust, 9,500 lines of Go, and 700 lines of TypeScript, all built by agents end-to-end.

Their benchmark for seriousness: if you haven't spent $1,000 per human engineer per day on AI compute, your software factory has room for improvement. That figure enables agents to run at a volume where the cost of compute is meaningful -- and it is often still cheaper than the human engineers it replaces.

The J-curve: why AI tools slow teams down before speeding them up

When organizations bolt an AI coding assistant onto an existing workflow, productivity dips before it rises -- forming a J-shaped curve. The dip occurs because the tool changes the workflow, but the workflow has not been redesigned around the tool. Developers spend time evaluating AI suggestions, correcting "almost right" code, context-switching between their own mental model and the model's output, and debugging subtle errors introduced by generated code that looks correct but is not.

For non-technical readers, think of it this way: imagine you hire a very fast but unreliable assistant. The assistant can type ten times faster than you, but you have to carefully check every single thing they produce. Initially, the overhead of checking actually makes you slower than just doing the work yourself. Only after you learn how to delegate effectively -- what to trust, what to verify, how to give clear instructions -- does the assistant's speed start to pay off.

GitHub Copilot illustrates this well. It has 20 million users and lab studies show 55% faster code completion on isolated tasks. But in production, the picture is more complicated: larger pull requests, higher review costs, and more security vulnerabilities introduced by generated code. One senior engineer summarized it sharply: "Copilot makes writing code cheaper but owning it more expensive."

The organizations seeing 25-30% or greater productivity gains are not the ones that installed Copilot, held a one-day seminar, and called it done. They are the ones that redesigned their entire development workflow around AI capabilities -- changing how they write specs, how they review code, what they expect from junior vs. senior engineers, and how their CI/CD pipelines catch the new category of errors that AI-generated code introduces. Most companies lack the stomach for this kind of end-to-end transformation, which is why most are stuck at the bottom of the J-curve.

Organizational structures built for human coders become friction in the AI era

Stand-up meetings, sprint planning, code review, QA teams -- every process in a traditional software organization exists because humans building software in teams need coordination structures. Stand-ups synchronize developers on the same codebase. Sprint planning manages human working memory limits. Code review catches human mistakes. QA exists because the people who build software cannot evaluate it objectively. Every one of these structures is a response to a human limitation.

When AI writes the code, these structures are no longer optional overhead; they become active friction. StrongDM's three-person team has no sprints, no stand-ups, and no Jira board. They write specifications and evaluate outcomes. The entire coordination layer that constitutes the "operating system" of a modern software organization -- the layer most managers spend 60% of their time maintaining -- simply does not exist.

The value of key roles is shifting. The engineering manager's value moves from "coordinate the team building the feature" to "define the specification clearly enough that agents build the feature." The program manager's value moves from "track dependencies between human teams" to "architect the pipeline of specs that flow through the factory." The critical skill shifts from coordination to articulation -- from making sure people row in the same direction to describing the direction precisely enough that machines can execute it.

Writing a specification detailed enough for an AI agent to implement correctly without human intervention is a fundamentally different skill than writing a spec for a human developer. Humans fill in gaps with judgment, context, and a Slack message asking "did you mean X or Y?" Machines build exactly what you described. If your description is ambiguous, you get software that fills the gaps with software guesses rather than customer-centric guesses. The bottleneck has moved from implementation speed to specification quality, and specification quality depends on how deeply you understand the system, the customer, and the problem.

The brownfield reality: you cannot dark-factory your way through legacy systems

Most of the software economy is not greenfield. It is existing systems accumulated over years or decades, running in production, serving real users, and carrying real revenue. CRUD applications, organically grown monoliths, CI/CD pipelines tuned to the quirks of a specific codebase, configuration knowledge that lives in the heads of the three people who remember why a particular environment variable is set to a particular value. These systems are their own specification -- no one ever wrote down the thousands of implicit decisions accumulated through patches, hotfixes, and "temporary" workarounds that became permanent.

For non-technical readers: a "brownfield" system is like an old building that has been renovated many times. The original blueprints are lost or inaccurate. Each renovation added new wiring, plumbing, and walls. Some of the building's quirks exist for reasons nobody remembers, but removing them might cause a collapse. You cannot hand an AI a set of instructions and say "rebuild this building" because no complete set of instructions exists. The building itself is the only accurate description of what it is.

The migration path starts with using AI at Level 2 or 3 to accelerate existing work (new features, bug fixes, refactoring), then using AI to document what the system actually does -- generating specs from code, building scenario suites that capture real behavior, and creating the holdout sets a future dark factory would need. Next comes redesigning CI/CD pipelines for AI-generated code at volume. Only then can new development shift to Level 4 or 5 patterns while the legacy system is maintained in parallel. This is a multi-month to multi-year process. The organizations that will get there fastest are not the ones with the fanciest tools but the ones with the deepest domain understanding and the discipline to invest in the unglamorous work of documenting what their systems actually do.

The talent reckoning: the junior developer pipeline is collapsing

Junior developer employment is dropping 9-10% within six quarters of widespread AI coding tool adoption, according to a 2025 Harvard study. In the UK, graduate tech roles fell 46% in 2024 with a further 53% drop projected by 2026. In the US, junior developer job postings have declined by 67%. The entry-level pipeline for software engineering is breaking down.

The implications go beyond unemployment. The career ladder in software engineering has always functioned as an apprenticeship model: juniors learn by writing simple features and fixing small bugs, seniors review and mentor them, and over 5-7 years a junior becomes a senior through accumulated experience. AI breaks this model at the bottom. If AI handles the simple features and bug fixes that juniors learn on, where do juniors develop their skills? If AI reviews code faster than a senior doing a PR review, where does mentorship begin? The ladder is being hollowed out: seniors at the top, AI at the bottom, and a thinning middle where learning used to happen.

Yet the need is not for fewer engineers but for better engineers. The bar is rising toward skills that have always been hardest to develop: systems thinking, customer intuition, the ability to hold an entire product in your head and reason about interactions, and the ability to write specifications clear enough for autonomous agents. "Adequate" is no longer a viable career position at any seniority level, because adequate is what the models already deliver.

Some organizations are moving toward a "medical residency" model where early-career developers learn by working alongside AI systems, reviewing AI output, and developing judgment about what is correct versus subtly wrong. For juniors entering the field, the advice is clear: lean into AI as an AI-native practitioner, develop generalist capabilities across domains rather than deep specialization in one narrow stack, and demonstrate that you can pick up a problem set and solve it quickly with AI across a wide range of use cases. The value of a specialist who knows everything about one technology but cannot reason about product implications is declining rapidly compared to a generalist who understands systems, users, and business constraints.

AI-native startups are redefining the economics of software companies

Cursor, the AI-native code editor, is past $500 million in annual recurring revenue with only a few dozen employees, operating at roughly $3.5 million in revenue per employee. The average SaaS company generates about $600,000 per employee. Midjourney similarly generates around half a billion in revenue with roughly 100 people. Lovable is well into hundreds of millions in ARR with a team scaling far behind its revenue growth. The top 10 AI-native startups average over $3 million in revenue per employee -- five to six times the SaaS industry average.

For non-technical readers, this means that AI-native companies are producing the same (or more) economic output as traditional software companies while employing a fraction of the people. This is not a fluke limited to one company; it is a repeatable pattern emerging across the industry. These companies do not have traditional engineering teams, product teams, QA teams, or DevOps teams. They are small groups of people who are exceptionally good at understanding user needs, translating those needs into clear specifications, and directing AI systems that handle the implementation.

The organizational implications are significant. The layers of coordination that exist to manage hundreds of engineers building a product disappear when engineering is done by agents. The middle management layer will either evolve into something fundamentally different or cease to exist. The only people who remain are those whose judgment cannot be automated: the ones who know what to build, for whom, and why -- and who have excellent "AI sense," a learned skill analogous to a rider's feel for directing a horse. For people in coordination-heavy roles -- middle management, release management, manual QA -- the imperative is to move toward developing with AI and rewriting their workflow around agents as quickly as possible.

The demand for software is exploding, not shrinking -- the constraint shifts to judgment

Every time the cost of computing has dropped -- mainframes to PCs, PCs to cloud, cloud to serverless -- the total amount of software the world produced did not stay flat. It exploded. New categories of software that were economically impossible at the old cost structure became viable, then ubiquitous, then essential. The cloud did not just make existing software cheaper to run; it created SaaS, mobile apps, streaming, real-time analytics, and hundreds of other categories that could not exist when you had to buy a rack of servers to ship anything.

The same dynamic is happening now at a scale that dwarfs previous transitions. Every company in every industry needs software, but most -- a regional hospital, a mid-market manufacturer, a family logistics company -- cannot afford to build what they need at current labor costs. A custom inventory system might cost over $500,000 and take more than a year. A patient portal integration might cost $300,000. These organizations make do with spreadsheets. As the cost of software production drops by an order of magnitude, that unmet need becomes addressable.

This is not merely a comforting rebuttal to job displacement fears. It is a structural observation about what happens when intelligence gets cheaper. The demand curve for every resource that has ever gotten dramatically cheaper -- compute, storage, bandwidth -- has never saturated. The constraint always moves to the next bottleneck. In this case, the new bottleneck is the judgment to know what to build and for whom.

The people who thrive are the ones who were always hardest to replace: those who understand customers deeply, think in systems, hold ambiguity, make decisions under uncertainty, and articulate what needs to exist before it exists. The dark factory does not replace those people. It amplifies them. It turns a great product thinker with five engineers into a great product thinker with unlimited engineering capacity. The constraint moves from "can we build it?" to "should we build it?" -- and "should we build it" has always been the harder and more interesting question.
