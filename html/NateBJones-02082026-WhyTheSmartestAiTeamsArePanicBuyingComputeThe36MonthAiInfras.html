Why the Smartest AI Teams Are Panic-Buying Compute: The 36-Month AI Infrastructure Crisis

NateBJones — 02/08/2026

Summary

The global economy has reorganized itself around AI capabilities, but the inference compute those capabilities depend on is now physically constrained, with no meaningful relief expected before 2028. Enterprise AI consumption is growing at roughly 10x annually, fueled by per-worker usage increases and the explosive growth of agentic systems, while the supply side is locked: DRAM fabrication takes 3-4 years, high-bandwidth memory is fully allocated to hyperscalers, and TSMC's advanced semiconductor nodes are booked solid. Hyperscalers like Google, Microsoft, Amazon, and Meta have locked up compute for years ahead and are prioritizing their own AI products (Gemini, Copilot, Alexa) over enterprise customers, creating a zero-sum conflict of interest. The result is an imminent pricing spike: memory costs alone are projected to add 40-60% to inference infrastructure costs in early 2026, and effective inference costs could double or triple within 18 months. Enterprises that fail to secure capacity now, build intelligent routing layers, and invest in token efficiency will find themselves locked out of the biggest technology race in history.

Key Points

AI demand is exponential and uncapped, with no ceiling in sight

A heavy AI user today consumes roughly 1 billion tokens per year, but the realistic ceiling is much higher, around 25 billion tokens or more. A single complex analysis task (such as reviewing 50 documents and synthesizing findings) can burn through half a million tokens. A full day of active coding can run into the millions. Research sessions with multiple iterations can reach 10 million tokens. There is, as the video argues, no natural demand limit for intelligence.

Three dynamics are accelerating this consumption. First, as AI models improve in areas like complex reasoning, each capability improvement unlocks non-linear new demand: tasks that were previously impractical suddenly become routine, and users find entirely new applications. Second, AI is no longer a standalone tool but is embedded across email, document editors, development environments, and CRM platforms, creating ambient, continuous token consumption across every touchpoint. Third, the shift to agentic systems, where AI orchestrates other AI in automated loops, represents an order-of-magnitude leap in consumption. A single agentic workflow can consume more tokens in an hour than a human generates in a month.

For non-technical readers: think of "tokens" as the basic units of text that AI processes (roughly a word or part of a word). Every time you ask an AI a question, every time it reads a document, every time it writes code, it is consuming tokens. When AI agents start calling other AI agents in chains, perhaps one agent researching, another summarizing, another checking quality, the token consumption multiplies dramatically. The video projects average per-worker consumption could hit 10 billion tokens annually within 18 months, with top users potentially exceeding 100 billion.

Enterprise costs will escalate from manageable to staggering

The math at enterprise scale is sobering. A 10,000-person organization consuming 1 billion tokens per worker at a blended API rate of $2 per million tokens spends $20 million per year on inference, expensive but manageable for a Fortune 500 company. At 10 billion tokens per worker, the same organization faces a $200 million annual bill. At 100 billion tokens per worker, which agentic systems could reach within 18 months, the cost balloons to $2 billion.

These projections assume stable pricing and available capacity, but neither assumption holds. Prices are rising, and capacity is constrained. Companies most at risk are those in the middle: too dependent on AI to abandon it, not large enough to secure dedicated allocation, and competing in markets where they cannot easily pass cost increases to customers. AI-native startups are especially exposed; some have publicly disclosed that AI costs already consume 10 percentage points of what was previously a 90% gross margin business.

For context, this is not like a typical IT cost increase where you can budget for a steady 5-10% annual rise. The combination of exponentially growing usage and spiking prices means organizations could face sudden, dramatic jumps in their AI bills. An AI project that was approved at one cost level may become financially unsustainable at twice or triple that cost, forcing difficult decisions about which AI initiatives to continue and which to cut.

Supply is physically constrained through 2028 across the entire hardware stack

The supply bottleneck runs through every layer of the hardware stack. At the memory level, server DRAM prices rose at least 50% through 2025 and are projected to rise another 55-60% quarter-over-quarter in Q1 2026. High-bandwidth memory (HBM), which is essential for running large AI models, is dominated by SK Hynix and entirely allocated to Nvidia, AMD, and hyperscalers. It cannot be purchased at any price. The three companies controlling 95% of global memory production (Samsung, SK Hynix, and Micron) are all reallocating manufacturing away from consumer products toward enterprise AI customers.

At the semiconductor fabrication level, TSMC manufactures virtually all of the world's most advanced AI chips. Their 5nm, 4nm, and 3nm process nodes are fully allocated, with Nvidia as their largest customer and Apple second. TSMC's capacity expansion is proceeding slowly: the Arizona fab will not reach full production until 2028, and new facilities in Japan and Germany are on similar timelines. Intel's 18A process, demonstrated at CES 2026, represents the first credible American alternative, but it is unproven at scale with limited capacity. Samsung's foundry has struggled with yields on advanced nodes. The result is that essentially all advanced AI chip production runs through a single company in Taiwan with no surge capacity and no near-term alternative.

For non-technical readers: building a new chip factory costs around $20 billion and takes 3-4 years to construct and ramp up to full production. This means decisions made today to invest in new capacity will not produce chips until approximately 2030. There is simply no way to rapidly increase the supply of the hardware AI depends on. This is fundamentally different from software constraints, where you can spin up new servers. The physical limitations of semiconductor manufacturing impose hard, multi-year delays that no amount of spending can accelerate.

Hyperscalers are hoarding compute and are not neutral partners

Google, Microsoft, Amazon, Meta, OpenAI, and Anthropic have all locked up GPU allocation for years in advance through multi-year purchase agreements worth tens of billions of dollars. Nvidia's H100 and Blackwell GPUs, the standard for data center AI, are sold out with lead times exceeding 6 months. The newer H200 and Blackwell GPUs are even more constrained, with initial production runs fully allocated to hyperscalers.

Critically, AWS, Azure, and Google Cloud are not neutral infrastructure providers. They are AI product companies that happen to sell infrastructure, and they compete directly with their enterprise customers. Google uses its compute to power Gemini, Microsoft uses its allocation for Copilot, and Amazon uses its allocation for AWS AI services. When compute is abundant, this conflict of interest is manageable; everyone gets served. When compute is scarce, as it is now, the conflict becomes zero-sum. Every GPU allocated to an enterprise customer is a GPU unavailable for the hyperscaler's own products.

The evidence of this prioritization is already visible. While API pricing has fallen over the past two years, rate limits have tightened. Enterprise customers report increasing difficulty getting allocation commitments for high-volume deployments. The hyperscalers are not being villains; they are being rational economic actors. Their AI products are strategic priorities with internal champions, while selling capacity to enterprises is a secondary business line. Enterprise CTOs need to internalize that cloud providers will not be reliable partners in this crisis. They are competitors who control the scarce resource enterprises need.

Pricing will spike, not rise gradually, due to structural market dynamics

When demand exceeds supply this severely and this structurally, markets do not clear smoothly. The video draws parallels to historical precedents: DRAM prices spiked 300% during the 2016 shortage, and GPU prices doubled during the crypto mining boom. Memory prices are notoriously volatile because supply is so inelastic; you simply cannot produce chips quickly.

The current situation has all the ingredients for a severe spike. Supply is inelastic (no new fabs for years). Demand is inelastic (enterprises are committed to AI and cannot easily back out). Information is asymmetric (hyperscalers understand exactly how constrained supply is, while most enterprises do not). And coordination is possible, even if unintentional, with only three major memory suppliers and one dominant GPU supplier. TrendForce projects that memory costs alone will add 40-60% to inference infrastructure costs in the first half of 2026.

For non-technical readers: this is not the kind of gradual price increase that businesses can absorb by adjusting budgets over time. When supply is fixed and demand keeps surging, prices can jump suddenly and dramatically, much like housing prices in a market with very few homes for sale. Companies that have not already secured their compute capacity will find themselves in a bidding war, paying enormous premiums, or simply unable to access the resources they need to run their AI systems.

Traditional IT planning frameworks are broken and must be replaced

Enterprise IT planning was designed for a world of predictable demand, stable technology, and available supply. None of these assumptions hold in the current AI environment. CapEx models, depreciation schedules, and multi-year procurement cycles all fail when demand is exponentially growing, model architectures and hardware capabilities are changing rapidly, and supply is severely constrained.

The video illustrates this with a concrete example: an enterprise purchases 1,000 AI workstations with NPU capabilities at $5,000 each, a $5 million capital investment depreciated over 4 years. By year two, per-worker consumption has grown 10x and the NPUs that were adequate for code completion cannot sustain agentic workflows consuming billions of tokens. The machines are not broken, they are simply obsolete. The enterprise is left choosing between continuing with inadequate hardware (losing competitive ground), purchasing new hardware (taking a write-down on the original investment), or leasing instead of buying (which transfers depreciation risk but at a premium).

Cloud committed-use agreements present their own trap. If you undercommit, you pay expensive on-demand rates for overages. If you overcommit, you waste money on unused capacity. Accurately predicting AI consumption in a dynamic environment with exponential growth curves is effectively impossible. Many enterprises are choosing to set a floor with committed agreements and absorb overages, accepting that they cannot predict consumption accurately. The key insight is that hardware should be treated as a consumable with a 2-year mental depreciation, not a long-lived capital asset.

The strategic playbook: secure capacity, build routing, invest in efficiency

Secure capacity before you need it. The highest-impact action an enterprise can take right now is obtaining contractual guarantees of inference throughput with SLAs for availability. The conversation with vendors should shift from "what is your price per million tokens" to "can you contractually guarantee us X billion tokens per day sustained with 99.9% availability." Volume guarantees matter more than unit pricing when supply is scarce.

Build an intelligent routing layer. The most durable competitive advantage in this environment is an intelligence layer that decides where workloads run. A sophisticated routing system optimizes for cost, manages capacity across providers, preserves optionality by abstracting infrastructure (making it trivial to switch providers), and enables negotiating leverage. This is not something that can be outsourced; it requires the ability to evaluate models on the fly, great observability, and a dedicated team. This routing layer is how enterprises maintain business independence from any single provider.

Invest in token efficiency. In a supply-constrained environment, efficiency becomes a competitive advantage. Every token you do not consume is capacity available for additional workloads. An enterprise that accomplishes the same task with 50% fewer tokens has effectively doubled its capacity. Strategies include using the smallest model capable for each task, designing well-crafted prompts that reduce token usage, implementing caching, using retrieval-augmented generation (embedding-based retrieval is orders of magnitude cheaper than raw inference), and applying quantization to enable smaller models to match larger model performance on specific tasks.

Diversify across the stack. Reduce dependence on any single player in the ecosystem. The enterprises that execute this playbook will be positioned to operate through the crisis and compete effectively when supply eventually evens out. Those that do not will find themselves capacity-constrained, cost-pressured, and falling behind. The window for action is open now but closing rapidly, and the decisions CTOs make in the next 6 months will separate winners from losers.
