Going Slower Feels Safer, But Your Domain Expertise Won't Save You Anymore

Summary

Nate B. Jones argues that AI is "collapsing" -- compressing, not destroying -- two critical dimensions of professional life: the horizontal distinction between knowledge-work roles and the temporal arc of career development. As AI agents become ubiquitous across every function (marketing, engineering, finance, legal, and beyond), the single skill that will matter most is the ability to orchestrate those agents effectively, layered on top of existing domain expertise. Traditional career planning, which assumed steady, multi-year accumulation of specialized knowledge, is being rendered obsolete by AI progress that doubles in capability on ever-shorter timescales. Jones urges professionals to stop waiting for AI to "mature" and instead lean in with curiosity and speed, comparing the learning process to riding a bicycle: going faster actually makes it easier to balance. With big tech committing over two trillion dollars in AI-related capital expenditure over the coming years, he concludes that there is no career path forward that does not run through AI.

Key Points

The Horizontal Collapse: All Knowledge-Work Roles Are Converging

Jones introduces the concept of a "horizontal collapse" of professional roles. Engineering, product management, marketing, analytics, design, and operations -- once sharply distinct career tracks with their own specialized skill sets -- are all converging toward a single meta-competency: orchestrating AI agents to accomplish work. By late 2026 or early 2027, he argues, no amount of deep domain expertise in any one of these areas will be sufficient on its own if you cannot also direct AI agents effectively.

For non-technical readers, "orchestrating AI agents" means giving instructions to AI-powered software tools that can perform tasks semi-autonomously -- drafting documents, analyzing data, writing code, managing project tickets, reviewing contracts, or handling customer inquiries. The skill is less about understanding how the AI works internally and more about knowing how to frame problems, provide the right context, and direct the AI toward useful outputs. Think of it as becoming a skilled conductor of an orchestra, where each instrument is an AI tool.

Jones points to concrete examples already visible in the workplace. Product managers who once spent weeks synthesizing customer feedback and writing specs now prompt AI models to draft those specs and use agents to analyze customer data. Legal teams compress weeks of contract review into hours. Finance teams use AI to build projections that previously took days. Customer success teams deploy agents handling 80-95% of initial support inquiries. What used to be fifty different specializations is converging into variations on one theme: humans directing AI with good knowledge and clear, software-shaped intent.

Critically, Jones emphasizes that domain expertise does not disappear -- it becomes foundational rather than differentiating. A senior professional's deep knowledge of marketing, finance, or engineering is still essential for directing AI well. But that knowledge alone, without the ability to channel it through AI tools, will no longer be enough to remain competitive.

Software-Shaped Intent: The Key Skill Most People Are Missing

One of the most distinctive ideas Jones puts forward is "software-shaped intent" -- the ability to think about problems the way software thinks about them, even if you are not a software engineer. When you ask an AI agent to do something, you need to frame your request in terms the agent can act on: Where is the data it needs to read? Where should it write the output? What tools does it have available? What does the workflow actually look like in the system it operates in?

For non-technical readers, this concept is easier to grasp with an analogy. Imagine you are asking an assistant to organize a filing cabinet. You would not just say "organize it" -- you would specify which drawers to use, what labels to apply, what order things should go in, and where to find the documents that need filing. Software-shaped intent is the same idea applied to AI: being specific about the inputs, outputs, and process so the agent can deliver something useful rather than something vague.

Jones stresses that this was historically a skill confined to product managers and engineers -- the people who already thought in terms of systems, data flows, and interfaces. But as AI agents become the primary way all knowledge workers get things done, this way of thinking is "coming out of the technical box." A marketer, a lawyer, a finance analyst, and a customer success manager all need to develop this intuition if they want to get the most out of AI tools.

At its core, software is about reading data, processing it, and presenting it in a useful way. The more you internalize that simple pattern, the better you will be at directing AI agents -- regardless of whether your background is technical or not. Jones sees this as one of the biggest gaps in how people currently interact with AI.

The Temporal Collapse: Career Timelines Are Compressing Into the Present

The second major collapse Jones identifies is temporal. The traditional career model assumed you had time: learn a skill, apply it for years, build expertise, get promoted, and eventually leverage that expertise in leadership. That model gave professionals breathing room to be strategic about when and where to invest their learning energy. Jones argues this assumption is now "catastrophically wrong."

He uses the SWE-bench coding benchmark as evidence. In 2023, AI systems could solve only 4% of problems on this benchmark. By 2025, that figure had reached roughly 90-95%, effectively saturating the entire benchmark. For non-technical readers, SWE-bench is a standardized test that measures how well AI can solve real-world software engineering tasks. The fact that AI went from barely functional to near-perfect on this test in just two years -- with the rate of improvement itself accelerating -- illustrates how quickly AI capabilities are advancing.

The practical implication is that the skills that will matter in 2027 are being defined right now by people who are actively engaging with AI today. If you wait until the technology "settles down" before investing in learning, you will find that early adopters have already built the workflows, established the norms, and captured the opportunities. They will have two years of compound learning while you are still figuring out the basics. There is no mature, stable state to wait for -- only a continuously steepening curve.

Jones also reframes how expertise itself works in this new reality. Under the old model, your expertise appreciated over time like an investment. Under the new model, expertise depreciates unless you continuously update it, and the rate of depreciation is accelerating. The half-life of any specific piece of AI knowledge is short and getting shorter, while the half-life of the habit of continuous learning is long and getting more durable. The meta-skill of ongoing adaptation is what compounds now, not any single piece of knowledge.

The Bicycle Analogy: Going Faster With AI Is Actually Safer

Jones draws a vivid analogy between learning AI and learning to ride a bicycle. When children first get on a bike, their instinct is to go as slowly as possible, believing that slower means safer. In reality, the opposite is true: a bicycle is more stable at higher speeds because of the gyroscopic effect and the rider's ability to make small, fluid corrections. Going too slowly makes balancing far harder and the experience much more frightening.

He applies this directly to AI adoption. Many professionals are approaching AI the way a nervous child approaches a bike -- inching forward, constantly braking, trying to fit AI cautiously into their existing workflows. Jones argues this is counterproductive. Going slowly forces you to overthink every adjustment, creates a constant feeling of instability, and keeps you in a state of anxiety. By contrast, leaning in aggressively -- trying new tools, experimenting with agents, pushing yourself to use AI for more and more of your work -- creates a sense of momentum and steadiness over time.

For people who feel overwhelmed by the pace of AI development, this reframe is important. Jones is not saying "panic and rush." He is saying that the feeling of being overwhelmed is partly a symptom of going too slowly. When you commit to engaging deeply and frequently, the patterns start to click into place. Your unconscious brain begins to recognize how AI systems work across different tools and contexts. The experience starts to feel stable -- not because the technology has slowed down, but because you have built enough momentum to keep your balance.

Follow the Money: The Biggest Capital Expenditure Project in Human History

For anyone who doubts whether AI is truly going to reshape every industry, Jones points to the financial commitments being made. Big tech's combined AI capital expenditure was close to half a trillion dollars in 2025 and is projected to exceed that in 2026. The five largest companies -- Amazon, Microsoft, Google, Meta, and Oracle -- collectively plan to invest at least $2 trillion in AI-related assets over the next four years. Jones calls this the biggest capital expenditure project in human history.

For non-technical readers, "capital expenditure" (capex) refers to the money companies spend on building long-term assets -- in this case, data centers, specialized AI chips, networking infrastructure, and the engineering teams to operate all of it. When a company spends this kind of money, it is not experimenting; it is making a strategic bet that it expects to define its business for the next decade or more. The sheer scale of these investments signals that the largest, most resourceful companies on Earth have concluded that AI will be the foundation of computing going forward.

Jones uses this point to counter the argument that AI might be a passing fad or that the hype will eventually die down. The money is already committed. The infrastructure is being built. The trajectory is set. Whether or not any individual professional chooses to engage, the industry is restructuring itself around AI. The only question is whether you position yourself on the side of that transformation or get caught on the wrong side of it.

AI Is Experiential: You Cannot Learn It From the Sidelines

A recurring theme throughout the video is that AI is fundamentally an experiential technology. Jones quotes a friend's line: "You do not get to learn to ride a horse by reading a book." Similarly, you do not learn to swim by sitting in a deck chair and watching the ocean. The only way to develop competence with AI is to use it -- repeatedly, across different contexts, and with increasing ambition.

This matters because many professionals are still in "research mode" with AI. They read articles, watch videos, attend a class or two, and then wait. Jones argues that this approach creates a false sense of preparation. The nature of AI tools -- their quirks, their capabilities, their failure modes, and the subtle art of prompting them effectively -- can only be internalized through hands-on practice. No amount of theoretical understanding substitutes for the tacit knowledge you build by actually using these systems to do real work.

He also addresses people who tried AI early (perhaps ChatGPT in 2022), were disappointed by hallucinations or limitations, and walked away. Jones is direct: you do not have time for that stance anymore. The tools have improved dramatically, and the gap between early adopters and holdouts is widening with every month. The invitation is simple -- pick one new AI tool or workflow, try it, learn from it, try the next thing, and keep leaning in. The patterns will solidify over time, and the process of engaging becomes self-reinforcing.

An Honest Off-Ramp: If AI Is Not for You, Choose a Different Path Intentionally

In a notably candid moment, Jones acknowledges that not everyone wants to ride this wave -- and he respects that. He mentions people he knows who have decided to exit tech entirely: opening a bookshop, pursuing carpentry, choosing a life away from screens. He frames this as a legitimate and even admirable decision, provided it is made intentionally rather than out of denial.

What he explicitly warns against is the middle ground: staying in an industry that is rapidly converging on AI while refusing to engage with it. That path, he argues, leads to frustration, declining relevance, and misery for everyone involved -- the individual and their colleagues. If your work involves a computer, it increasingly involves AI. There is no version of a screen-based knowledge-work career in the near future that does not require meaningful AI competence.

This is an important nuance because it reframes the conversation away from "everyone must love AI" toward something more honest: recognize what is happening, assess whether you want to be part of it, and make a clear choice either way. The worst outcome is drifting -- neither fully engaging nor fully disengaging -- because that guarantees you get the downsides of both without the benefits of either.

Curiosity as a Competitive Advantage

Jones closes on an encouraging note, arguing that the single most powerful stance you can adopt right now is curiosity. He has observed "dozens and dozens" of people across widely different fields -- healthcare, tech, finance, engineering, product, and even small-town community building -- who chose to approach AI with genuine curiosity rather than fear or resistance. Without exception, he says, that choice took them farther than they expected.

There is a neuroscience basis for this claim, though Jones only touches on it briefly: curiosity literally opens up the brain. Research in learning science shows that when people are in a curious, exploratory state, they absorb and retain information more effectively than when they are anxious or defensive. In a landscape where continuous learning is the primary meta-skill, the emotional posture you bring to AI adoption is not a soft, feel-good detail -- it is a material competitive advantage.

His practical advice is straightforward: pick something new -- Claude Code, Lovable, a different way of working with your chatbot, whatever is unfamiliar -- and try it. Then try the next thing. Then lean in a little farther. Each step builds on the last, and over time the patterns of how AI works across different systems solidify in your unconscious understanding. The process of going faster with AI is self-reinforcing: the more you engage, the steadier it feels, and the steadier it feels, the faster you can go.
